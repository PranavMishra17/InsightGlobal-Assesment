Condition Overview

Parkinson's disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder and the second most common neurodegenerative condition after Alzheimer's disease [22]. It is characterized by motor symptoms including tremor, bradykinesia, and rigidity, as well as a broad spectrum of non-motor manifestations such as rapid eye movement sleep disorder, anosmia, constipation, depression, cognitive impairment, and pain [23] [48]. Male sex and advancing age are independent risk factors [16]. In most populations, 3–5% of cases are explained by genetic causes, with the remainder attributed to complex interactions between environmental and genetic factors [17]. The pathogenesis centers on the deposition of aggregated α-synuclein and the loss of dopaminergic neurons, though recent models propose that initial pathology may start either in the brain (olfactory bulb or amygdala, "brain-first" subtype) or in the enteric nervous system ("body-first" subtype) [20] [54]. The rising global prevalence of PD resembles characteristics of a pandemic, with marked variations across time, geography, ethnicity, age, and sex [17] [19]. Prevalence has increased beyond what demographic changes alone would explain, with a doubling of cases reported between 1995 and 2015 and a further doubling projected [48] [19]. PD is increasingly recognized as a multisystem disorder with prodromal non-motor symptoms appearing years or even decades before classic motor manifestations [51] [21]. Two biological definition systems proposed in 2024 both center on the presence or absence of pathological α-synuclein, which could reshape how PD is classified and diagnosed going forward [41] [24]. No pharmacological agents currently prevent PD onset or definitively halt its progression, making disease-modifying therapies an urgent unmet need [25] [41].

Current Standard of Care

The cornerstone of PD pharmacotherapy remains dopamine replacement. Carbidopa/levodopa is FDA-approved for the treatment of Parkinson's disease, post-encephalitic parkinsonism, and symptomatic parkinsonism, and functions by allowing patients to benefit from lower doses of levodopa while reducing nausea and vomiting [40]. According to the American Academy of Neurology (AAN) guideline (originally published 2021, reaffirmed February 2025), treatment with levodopa provides superior benefit in reducing motor symptoms compared to dopamine agonists or MAO-B inhibitors [41]. Non-ergot dopamine agonists represent an important adjunctive or alternative class. Pramipexole dihydrochloride extended-release tablets are FDA-approved for the treatment of Parkinson's disease [36]. Ropinirole hydrochloride is similarly FDA-approved for PD treatment [37]. While dopamine agonists are less likely to cause dyskinesia than levodopa, they carry higher risks of impulse-control disorders (compulsive gambling, eating, shopping, or sexual activity) and hallucinations [41]. Updated levodopa dose equivalency conversion formulae have been systematically reviewed to enable cross-comparison of drug regimens across clinical trials [53]. Beyond pharmacotherapy, deep brain stimulation (DBS) is a widely used therapy for PD, and the German Society of Neurology (DGN) 2024 guideline includes recommendations for non-oral therapies such as pump therapy and DBS alongside tailored pharmacological combinations for motor complications [41] [34]. Updated guidelines also cover management of cognitive, affective, psychotic, and autonomic symptoms [41]. The 2024 Lancet series emphasizes that although dopamine replacement remains central, the onset of motor fluctuations and dyskinesia may require a range of medical and surgical approaches from a multidisciplinary team [41]. Exercise and rehabilitation are increasingly recognized as essential components of PD management. Aerobic exercise has been shown in randomized controlled trials to attenuate motor symptom progression and alter brain function and structure in the corticostriatal sensorimotor network [26] [25]. Treadmill training and physiotherapy both improve dual-task gait performance [27], and clinical Pilates, LSVT BIG protocols, augmented/virtual reality gait training, and motor-cognitive training all demonstrate benefits for balance and gait [28] [29] [33] [52]. Home physical therapy and telerehabilitation have been compared as delivery modalities to improve motor function and quality of life [32].

Emerging Treatments in Development

The PD clinical pipeline is robust, with 136 active Phase 1–3 drug therapy trials registered on ClinicalTrials.gov as of January 31, 2024. Of these, 76 (56%) target symptomatic treatment and 60 (44%) are designated disease-modifying. More than half (58%) are in Phase 2, followed by Phase 1 (30%) and Phase 3 (12%), underscoring that only a limited number of disease-modifying candidates are transitioning to late-stage development [41]. **Disease-Modifying Candidates — α-Synuclein-Targeting:** Prasinezumab, an investigational anti-α-synuclein monoclonal antibody developed by Hoffmann-La Roche, has completed a Phase 2 randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in participants with early PD on stable symptomatic medication [13]. It has now advanced to a Phase 3 trial (currently recruiting) evaluating efficacy, safety, and pharmacokinetics in participants with early-stage PD on stable symptomatic levodopa monotherapy [15]. **GLP-1 Receptor Agonists:** Lixisenatide, a GLP-1 receptor agonist used for diabetes, showed neuroprotective properties in a mouse model and was tested in a Phase 2 randomized, placebo-controlled trial in 156 participants with early PD. At 12 months, motor disability (MDS-UPDRS Part III) worsened by 3.04 points in the placebo group but was essentially unchanged (−0.04 points) in the lixisenatide group, a statistically significant difference of 3.08 points (95% CI 0.86–5.30; P=0.007), though gastrointestinal side effects were noted [35] [41]. Additionally, a Phase 3 trial of exenatide (investigational, another GLP-1 receptor agonist) with 96-week exposure was conducted in the UK to confirm earlier positive results [41]. **Cell-Based Therapies:** A published Phase I/II trial of iPSC-derived dopaminergic cells for PD demonstrated that initial cell therapies using fetal tissues showed promise but had complications and ethical concerns, motivating the development of iPSC-based approaches [30]. XS411, an investigational human allogeneic iPSC-derived dopaminergic neural progenitor cell injection developed by XellSmart Bio-Pharmaceutical, is in a Phase I/II clinical study for early-onset Parkinson's disease using a 3+3 dose-escalation design [9]. Separately, Mass General Brigham treated its first patient in September 2024 in a Phase 1 autologous iPSC-derived cell therapy trial [41]. **Symptomatic and Adjunctive Investigational Therapies:** Lenrispodun (investigational), sponsored by Intra-Cellular Therapies, is being evaluated in a Phase 2 randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial as adjunctive therapy for motor fluctuations (wearing-off symptoms) in PD patients on levodopa [3]. BHV-8000 (investigational), sponsored by Biohaven Therapeutics, is in a Phase 2 trial for adults with early PD to assess efficacy, safety, and tolerability [5]. CTC-413 (investigational), a combination of pramipexole ER with aprepitant from Chase Therapeutics, is in a Phase 2 study for parkinsonian-type disorders [6]. **Repurposed Agents Under Investigation:** Donepezil (FDA-approved for Alzheimer's disease) is being studied in a Phase 2 trial at the University of Virginia to investigate the cholinergic contribution to gait dysfunction in PD [4]. Levetiracetam (an anti-epileptic drug) is in a Phase 2 proof-of-concept trial at the University of Queensland targeting memory problems in PD, with neuroimaging used to assess effects on hippocampal circuits implicated in dementia risk [11]. Pyridostigmine bromide is in a Phase 2 trial at the University of Vermont Medical Center for the treatment of constipation in PD, a common non-motor symptom with no current standardized treatment algorithm [46]. Fexofenadine is being explored as adjuvant therapy alongside levodopa/carbidopa in a Phase 2 trial at Tanta University [8]. Tributyrin, a butyrate supplement, is in a Phase 1/2 trial evaluating its effects on cognition, walking, and balance in PD patients with cognitive impairments [12]. Fecal microbiota transplantation has been tested in a randomized, placebo-controlled trial to assess its impact on microbiota engraftment in PD patients, given evidence linking gut dysbiosis to PD occurrence and progression [31]. **Adaptive Deep Brain Stimulation:** A blinded randomized feasibility trial compared chronic adaptive (feedback-controlled) DBS versus conventional stimulation in PD, exploring whether dynamic responsiveness to changing neural states could improve therapeutic effectiveness [34]. A separate Phase 4 mechanistic study is investigating personalized real-time DBS in patients undergoing standard-of-care implantation [14]. **Neuroinflammation and Biomarker Imaging:** Multiple trials are advancing neuroinflammation imaging biomarkers. [18F]DPA-714 PET is being used in both a Phase 1 study at UAB to measure activated brain microglia/macrophages [2] and a Phase 2 multicenter study assessing the link between microglial activation and dopaminergic denervation kinetics in early PD [7]. [18F]AV-133 PET imaging is under longitudinal evaluation in prodromal PD participants through the PPMI initiative to track disease progression over 24 months [1].

Key Companies and Institutions

The Parkinson's disease landscape involves a mix of major pharmaceutical companies, specialty biotechnology firms, academic medical centers, and non-profit foundations. Hoffmann-La Roche is the most prominent large pharmaceutical player in the current pipeline, sponsoring both the Phase 2 and Phase 3 trials of prasinezumab (investigational), the furthest-advanced anti-α-synuclein antibody in development [13] [15]. Intra-Cellular Therapies, Inc. is developing lenrispodun (investigational) for motor fluctuations in PD [3]. Biohaven Therapeutics Ltd. is advancing BHV-8000 (investigational) in early PD [5]. Chase Therapeutics Corporation is evaluating CTC-413 (investigational) for parkinsonian disorders [6]. XellSmart Bio-Pharmaceutical (Suzhou) Co., Ltd. is pursuing iPSC-derived cell therapy (XS411, investigational) for early-onset PD [9]. Academic and medical institutions are leading multiple investigator-sponsored trials. The University of Virginia is studying donepezil for gait dysfunction [4]; the University of Queensland is testing levetiracetam for PD-related cognitive impairment [11]; the University of Vermont Medical Center is evaluating pyridostigmine for PD-associated constipation [46]; Tanta University is investigating fexofenadine as adjuvant therapy [8]; and Teachers College, Columbia University is conducting a rehabilitation trial for airway protection in PD [10]. The University of Alabama at Birmingham and Nantes University Hospital are advancing neuroinflammation PET imaging biomarker studies [2] [7]. The Michael J. Fox Foundation for Parkinson's Research plays a central role in biomarker development, sponsoring the PPMI [18F]AV-133 prodromal imaging study [1]. Mass General Brigham has initiated a first-in-human autologous iPSC-derived dopaminergic cell therapy trial [41].

Recent Developments

Several significant developments have shaped the PD landscape in 2024–2025: **GLP-1 Receptor Agonist Class Emerges as a Disease-Modification Candidate:** The Phase 2 LIXIPARK trial of lixisenatide (investigational for PD) published in the New England Journal of Medicine in April 2024 demonstrated that participants with early PD receiving lixisenatide showed essentially no motor disability progression over 12 months (MDS-UPDRS Part III change of −0.04 points), compared to 3.04 points of worsening in the placebo group (difference 3.08 points; 95% CI 0.86–5.30; P=0.007) [35] [41]. This has been followed by a Phase 3 trial of exenatide (investigational for PD), another GLP-1 receptor agonist, with 96-week exposure, published in The Lancet in February 2025 [41]. These results have positioned the GLP-1 agonist class as one of the most closely watched disease-modification strategies in PD. **Prasinezumab Advances to Phase 3:** Following Phase 2 results, Hoffmann-La Roche has initiated a Phase 3 randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of prasinezumab (investigational) in participants with early-stage PD on stable levodopa monotherapy [15]. This represents one of the few α-synuclein-targeting therapies to reach Phase 3, a stage where the overall PD pipeline remains thin (only 12% of active trials are Phase 3) [41]. **iPSC-Derived Cell Therapy Milestones:** A Phase I/II trial of iPSC-derived dopaminergic cells for Parkinson's disease was published in Nature in 2025, marking a landmark in regenerative approaches [30]. Separately, a Phase I/II trial of XS411, an allogeneic iPSC-derived dopaminergic neural progenitor cell injection, is recruiting for early-onset PD [9], and Mass General Brigham treated its first patient with an autologous iPSC-derived cell therapy in September 2024 [41]. **Adaptive Deep Brain Stimulation Shows Feasibility:** A blinded randomized crossover pilot trial published in Nature Medicine in 2024 demonstrated the feasibility of chronic adaptive DBS, which uses feedback control to dynamically respond to changing clinical and neural states. The trial explored whether this approach could improve therapeutic effectiveness over conventional stimulation [34]. **Evolving Disease Definitions and Biomarker Strategies:** Two new biological definition systems for PD were proposed in early 2024, both centering on the presence or absence of pathological α-synuclein. This shift toward biologically based classification is intended to enable earlier detection—ideally in the prodromal phase—and to improve clinical trial design for disease-modifying therapies [41] [24]. Longitudinal imaging studies using [18F]AV-133 PET in prodromal PD participants (PPMI initiative) [1] and neuroinflammation imaging with [18F]DPA-714 [2] [7] are actively advancing biomarker development for tracking disease progression and treatment response. **Gut-Brain Axis and Microbiome Interventions:** A randomized, placebo-controlled trial of fecal microbiota transplantation in PD patients has produced clinical results, reflecting the growing evidence base linking gut dysbiosis to PD pathogenesis [31]. The body-first versus brain-first model of PD, updated in 2024, posits that enteric nervous system pathology may initiate the disease in a subset of patients, reinforcing the rationale for gut-targeted interventions [54]. **Exercise and Rehabilitation Evidence Strengthens:** A 2022 randomized controlled trial demonstrated that aerobic exercise alters brain function and structure in the corticostriatal sensorimotor network in PD, providing a mechanistic basis for exercise-related clinical improvements [26]. A 2024 review emphasized that no pharmacological agents currently prevent PD evolution or progression, and that exercise remains one of the most promising interventions with potential to modify disease trajectory [25]. New evidence in 2024 also compared home physical therapy with telerehabilitation modalities for motor function improvement in PD [32]. **Repurposed Drug Investigations Expand:** Multiple repurposed agents are under active Phase 2 investigation for non-motor PD symptoms, including levetiracetam for dementia risk reduction [11], pyridostigmine for constipation [46], donepezil for gait dysfunction [4], and tributyrin for cognitive impairments [12]. These trials address critical unmet needs, as non-motor symptoms are often among the most disabling and treatment-refractory features of PD [41].
